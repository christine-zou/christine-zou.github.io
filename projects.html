Redesigning Soccer Cleats to Reduce ACL Tears in Female Athletes

Abstract — This research study aims to redesign soccer cleats for female soccer players to reduce the likelihood of anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) tears. Females are up to eight times more likely to tear their ACL in comparison to males, and soccer cleats are mainly designed for men with women's cleats lacking comfort and performance. We propose an alternative design by varying the density of the outsole to relieve pressure off the knee. Our design redistributes the forces on the foot to achieve an optimal valgus and knee flexion angle, putting female soccer players less at risk, thus reducing the forces that lead to ACL injuries. Seven different outsole density patterns were designed based on the Nike Premier FG 3 and then printed with Ultimakers out of thermoplastic polyurethane using support blockers in Cura. Each pattern was tested on five athletic young adult females. The knee flexion and valgus angles were measured using a virtual reality tracking system as the subjects performed actions of walking, running, and jumping.

Where things went wrong!

During our initial testing, I identified that the transmission ratio was too low, casuing our belt to slip under load. Our system lacked sufficient torque to overcome the mechanical resistance so we first tried to increase the tension in the belt as shown in the images below. However, the tension required for stable, reliable operation was still greater than what this configuration could maintain.

Belt Tensioning Test Position 1
Belt Tensioning Test Position 2

As my teammates explored different approaches to maintain sufficient belt tension over the following days, I focused on developing and refining the Arduino control code so we could transition smoothly into testing once the system was ready. Yet, by the night before our final demonstration, the assembly remained nonfunctional. Determined to resolve the issue, I combed through the lab's spare components and found a compatible gear with the same tooth profile but a smaller diameter to increase our transmission ratio. After carefully modifying it by drilling out the press-fit pin (shown on the right), we replaced the original driving gear, allowing for full motion to the linkage and achieving reliable performance just in time.
